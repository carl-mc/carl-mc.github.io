Shaping States into Nations: The Effects of Ethnic Geography on State Borders

Carl Müller-Crepon, Guy Schivtz, and Lars-Erik Cederman
August 2020

Abstract

Borders are the defining elements of states, but their origins have rarely been studied empirically. In this paper, we focus on ethnic geography and nationalism as drivers of border formation in Europe since the 19th century. We argue that the rise of nationalism created growing popular pressures to redraw political boundaries along ethnic lines, ultimately causing state borders to align increasingly with the ethnic map. To test this argument, we rely on a new Probabilistic Spatial Partition Model and collect spatial data on Europe’s ethnic geography since 1855. Our findings consistently show that the presence of an ethnic boundary between two locations strongly increases the probability that these locations are, or will become, separated by an international border. Using information on areas subject to secessionism, additional results show that secession is an important driver through which ethnic geography shapes states. Lastly, we find similar dynamics of border change in Asia since the 1960s but not in Africa and the Americas. Our results highlight the endogenous formation of nation-states in Europe and beyond.
