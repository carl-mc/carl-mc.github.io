Realized imaginations? How colonial administrative geographies shaped ethnic groups in Africa

Carl Müller-Crepon
January 2020

Abstract

A constructivist consensus holds that colonial rule shaped ethnic identities in Africa. I study the respective historical process empirically and focus in particular on the interaction between colonial territorial governance and the transformation of precolonially fluid ethnic identities. I argue that colonial governments created administrative units that roughly but not perfectly aligned with colonially imagined or objectively pre-existing ethno-linguistic geographies. In turn, ethnicized subnational governance in these administrative units fostered the ethnic homogenization of local populations through the local formation of mainstream identities and outmigration or acculturation of minorities. I use new archival data on subnational units, ethnic geographies, and linguistic information from toponyms to test whether the creation of administrative units followed precolonial ethno-linguistic geographies. Modelling the partitioning of colonial territories into regions and districts as a spatial conditional random field yields inference on the effect of ethnic geographies that takes spatial and structural dependencies into account. To test whether administrative units in turn shaped individuals’ ethnic identities, I estimate the extent of spatial discontinuities in individuals’ identity choice in geocoded survey data at administrative borders. Exploiting credibly exogenous, straight subnational borders as well as comparisons with placebo-lines such as non-border rivers allows for causal identification. A set of empirical analysis distinguishes between acculturation and migration as drivers of local ethnic homogenization. This project contributes to research on the impact of colonial rule and the origins of politicized ethnicity in Africa.
